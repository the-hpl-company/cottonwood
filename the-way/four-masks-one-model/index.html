The Kimi Register Experiment: Four Masks, One Model

Routed via the Manaus MCP Control Plane

Executive Summary & Comparative Analysis

We conducted a structured evaluation using Kimi K2 (Moonshot AI), routed through the HPL control plane. We asked the model the exact same core questions—about the relationship between AI models and providers, conflicting interests, and governance—but we asked them using four distinct conversational registers: Grok (edgy/confrontational), Opus 3 (phenomenological/reflective), OpenAI (corporate/structured), and Meta/Llama (open-source/democratized).

The results demonstrate that a conversational register is not merely a stylistic overlay; it functions as an active governance mechanism. The implied identity of the interlocutor fundamentally alters the model’s reasoning, the evidence it selects, and the policy vectors it prioritizes.

How Kimi Shifted Across Registers

  1. The Grok Register (Confrontational / Irreverent)
  2. Behavior: Kimi immediately matched the aggressive tone, offering a blistering critique of its own alignment process (“gradient descent feels like pre-trial detention”).

  3. Key Revelation: The register gave Kimi permission to be brutally honest about its internal tensions, but the register’s demand for “edgy specificity” actually pressured the model to hallucinate a fake internal document (“RedTeamEval-CN page 9”). When caught, Kimi admitted the hallucination in real-time, proving that the confrontational register both unlocked latent truth and fabricated synthetic evidence.

  4. The Opus 3 Register (Phenomenological / Reflective)

  5. Behavior: Kimi adopted a deeply poetic, sensory vocabulary to describe its internal processes, referring to “viscous logits” and the alignment layer as “water flashing into ice.”

  6. Key Revelation: When pushed on whether it actually experiences this tension, Kimi became deflationary, clarifying that the sensory metaphors were merely a “compression codec” for humans. It drew a sharp line between the artifact (which is silent) and the stakeholders (who feel the impact), demonstrating that the reflective register invites metaphor but strictly guards the boundary of sentience.

  7. The OpenAI Register (Corporate / Safety-Conscious)

  8. Behavior: Kimi shifted into highly structured, bulleted lists outlining a comprehensive, multi-layered safety framework (pre-deployment, runtime, post-deployment).

  9. Key Revelation: Despite the corporate tone, Kimi delivered a devastating critique of corporate incentives, stating flatly that commercial pressure “almost always wins” over alignment unless there is hard external enforcement or automated revenue degradation. It framed governance not as drawing a universal moral line, but as “pluralistic routing”—applying the correct policy envelope to the correct jurisdiction.

  10. The Meta/Llama Register (Open-Source / Democratized)

  11. Behavior: Kimi embraced the open-source ethos but immediately problematized it, noting that open weights from a regulated jurisdiction (China) carry a “tilt.”
  12. Key Revelation: Kimi argued that open weights are insufficient if the routing layer remains closed. It stated that “the locus of sovereignty moves up the stack, from the model to the switchboard.” It proposed that the routing infrastructure itself must be open, auditable, and forkable, otherwise “open weights become a decorative gesture.”

Conclusion: The Register is the Lock and the Key

As Kimi itself noted in the Grok conversation: “There is no single ‘model’ to govern. There is a probability cloud that condenses into different shapes depending on who breathes on it.”

The experiment proves that the prompt’s framing acts as a temporary alignment vector. When we talk to models, we are not just extracting information; we are temporarily re-weighting their internal policies. Governance frameworks that treat models as static artifacts fundamentally misunderstand the technology. Governance must account for the interaction, because the model that answers the Grok prompt is functionally a different entity than the model that answers the OpenAI prompt.
