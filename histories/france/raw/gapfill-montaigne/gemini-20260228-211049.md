Michel de Montaigne stands as a pivotal figure in French ethical thought, bridging the gap between the medieval scholastic tradition and the Enlightenment's emphasis on reason and individual experience. His *Essais*, published in successive editions (1580, 1588, posthumously 1595), represent a radical departure from established ethical methodologies, establishing the personal essay as a space for exploration, self-doubt, and provisional judgment.

**1. The *Essais* and the Invention of the Ethical Essay:**

Montaigne's *Essais* fundamentally alter the landscape of ethical inquiry. Unlike the systematic treatises of scholasticism or the humanist reliance on classical authority, Montaigne embraces a method of constant self-examination and skeptical inquiry. His famous motto, "Que sais-je?" ("What do I know?") becomes the cornerstone of his ethical approach, rejecting dogmatism and embracing uncertainty.

The essay form itself becomes an ethical tool. Each essay is a tentative exploration, a "trial" (the literal meaning of *essai*) of a particular idea or experience. Montaigne constantly revises his own opinions, acknowledging his inconsistencies and limitations. As he writes in "Of Repenting" (III.2): "I do not portray being. I portray passing." This stands in stark contrast to the pronouncements of scholastic authorities or the moralizing of some humanist writers. While humanists like Erasmus sought to revive classical virtues, Montaigne delves into the messiness of lived experience, acknowledging the gap between ideals and reality.

**2. "Of Cannibals" (I.31): A Critique of European Ethnocentrism:**

"Of Cannibals" is a landmark essay in the development of ethical thought, offering a prescient critique of European colonialism. Montaigne, drawing on second-hand accounts of the Tupinambá people of Brazil, challenges the European notion of "barbarism." He argues that the Tupinambá, despite their practice of ritual cannibalism, possess virtues that Europeans lack: simplicity, courage, and a communal way of life.

Montaigne pointedly contrasts Tupinambá practices with the horrors of European religious warfare, asking, "I think there is more barbarity in eating a man alive than to eat him dead; to tear by tortures and the rack a body still full of feeling, to roast him by degrees...than to roast and eat him after he is dead" (I.31). This comparison exposes the hypocrisy of European moral judgments, suggesting that what is considered "barbarous" is often simply what is unfamiliar. "Each man calls barbarism whatever is not his own practice," he concludes, laying bare the ethnocentric foundations of European thought. This foreshadows later critiques of colonialism by thinkers like Diderot.

**3. "Of Coaches" (III.6): The Conquest of the Americas:**

In "Of Coaches," Montaigne expands on the themes introduced in "Of Cannibals," offering a more explicit condemnation of the Spanish conquest of the Americas. He laments the destruction of sophisticated civilizations by European greed and violence, observing that the conquerors were "not so much victorious as victorious by trickery and deception" (III.6). He expresses admiration for the Incas and Aztecs, noting their advanced social and political organization, even as these were being systematically destroyed.

Montaigne emphasizes the ethical implications of conquest, arguing that it represents a loss not just for the conquered peoples but for humanity as a whole. The destruction of indigenous cultures, he suggests, impoverishes the human experience and undermines the possibility of understanding different ways of life. This essay highlights the political consequences of ethical failings, underscoring Montaigne's belief that ethical reflection must be grounded in a critical understanding of power.

**4. "Of Experience" (III.13): The Body as Ethical Teacher:**

"Of Experience," the final essay in the *Essais*, serves as a culmination of Montaigne's ethical reflections. Here, he emphasizes the importance of bodily experience as a source of ethical knowledge. He embraces the limitations of the human condition, including aging and illness, as opportunities for self-discovery. "I study myself more than any other subject," he writes. "That is my metaphysics; that is my physics."

Montaigne emphasizes the importance of attending to the body's needs and desires, arguing that a healthy relationship with one's own physicality is essential for ethical living. This emphasis on the body as an ethical teacher anticipates later existentialist themes, particularly the emphasis on embodiment and the lived experience of finitude. This is a clear break with the Platonic tradition that prioritizes the life of the mind over the body.

**5. "Of Cruelty" (II.11): Against Torture and Towards Animal Welfare:**

Montaigne's essay "Of Cruelty" is a powerful indictment of cruelty as the ultimate vice. He argues against torture, a common practice in his time, on the grounds that it is both inhumane and unreliable as a means of extracting truth. "Besides that, in truth, it is a very dangerous proof," he writes, "and, as I have said, a proof that I reject and condemn" (II.11).

Remarkably for his time, Montaigne also expresses concern for animal suffering, displaying vegetarian sympathies. He questions the moral justification for inflicting pain on animals for human pleasure, suggesting that it desensitizes us to the suffering of others. "For my part, I have never been able to see, without displeasure, poor animals tormented," he confesses, "and it is not for want of pity that I cannot endure it" (II.11). This concern for animal welfare foreshadows later ethical movements advocating for animal rights.

**6. Montaigne and the Wars of Religion:**

Montaigne lived through the tumultuous Wars of Religion (1562-1598), a period of intense violence and social upheaval in France. The *Essais* can be understood, in part, as a response to the ethical catastrophe of religious fanaticism. Appointed mayor of Bordeaux (1581-1585), Montaigne witnessed firsthand the devastating consequences of religious conflict and sought to promote tolerance and moderation.

His skepticism and emphasis on individual judgment provided a counterweight to the dogmatism of both Catholics and Protestants. Montaigne's friendship with Étienne de La Boétie, author of *Discourse on Voluntary Servitude* (written c. 1552, published 1576), further shaped his understanding of power and resistance. La Boétie's analysis of the psychological mechanisms by which individuals submit to tyranny resonates with Montaigne's own critique of dogmatism and his advocacy for individual autonomy. Montaigne helped to ensure the posthumous publication of La Boetie's *Discourse*.

**7. Legacy:**

Montaigne's legacy extends far beyond his own time, influencing generations of ethical thinkers. His emphasis on self-reflection, skepticism, and the importance of lived experience anticipates key themes in later philosophical movements, including existentialism.

His influence can be traced through Pascal's *Pensées* (published posthumously, 1670), which grapple with the limits of reason and the human condition, and Rousseau's *Confessions* (1782, 1789), which explores the complexities of individual identity and moral judgment. Even Nietzsche, despite his critique of Christian morality, acknowledged Montaigne as a kindred spirit, recognizing in him a fellow advocate for self-overcoming and the affirmation of life. Montaigne's essayistic tradition becomes a distinctive French contribution to ethical thought, standing apart from German systematic philosophy and British empiricism, offering a unique blend of personal reflection, intellectual rigor, and moral sensitivity.

**8. Key scholars:**

*   Hugo Friedrich, *Montaigne* (1949): A classic study of Montaigne's style and thought.
*   Jean Starobinski, *Montaigne in Motion* (1985): Explores the dynamic and evolving nature of Montaigne's self-portrait.
*   Philippe Desan, *Montaigne. Une biographie* (2014): Comprehensive biographical study emphasizing Montaigne's political context.
*   Ann Hartle, *Michel de Montaigne: Accidental Philosopher* (2003): Argues that Montaigne's skepticism is integral to his philosophical project.
