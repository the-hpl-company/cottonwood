**1. Medieval Jewry: Rashi and the Tosafists**

The presence of a vibrant Jewish community in medieval France, particularly in the Champagne region, gave rise to a distinct ethical and intellectual tradition that ran parallel to Christian Scholasticism. Rabbi Shlomo Yitzchaki, known as Rashi (1040-1105), based in Troyes, is the towering figure of this tradition. His commentaries on the Talmud and the Hebrew Bible are foundational texts of Jewish learning. Rashi's method, characterized by its clarity, concision, and emphasis on the *peshat* (plain sense) of the text, aimed to make Jewish law and lore accessible to a broad audience. Rashi's approach prioritized understanding the text in its immediate context, providing clear explanations of legal principles and narratives. This focus on accessibility and direct engagement with the text distinguished it from the more abstract and philosophical approaches of some Christian Scholastics.

Following Rashi, the Tosafists (12th-13th centuries), many of whom were his descendants and intellectual heirs, continued this tradition of Talmudic commentary in northern France. Unlike Rashi's relatively concise explanations, the Tosafists engaged in more extensive critical analysis, comparing different Talmudic passages, raising objections, and proposing alternative interpretations. Their commentaries, known as *Tosafot*, are characterized by their dialectical approach, seeking to resolve contradictions and harmonize different perspectives within the Talmud. This tradition of rigorous textual analysis and legal reasoning contributed to a sophisticated ethical framework that addressed a wide range of practical and moral questions, from business ethics to family law. The Tosafists demonstrate a commitment to intellectual rigor and engagement with complex issues that mirrored aspects of Scholasticism, albeit rooted in a distinct set of texts and traditions. This parallel tradition of rabbinic scholarship offered a distinct ethical framework grounded in Jewish law and custom, shaping the moral lives of Jewish communities in France and beyond.

**2. Expulsions and Returns**

The history of Jews in France during the late medieval and early modern periods was marked by periods of integration and expulsion. The expulsion of 1306, decreed by Philip IV ("the Fair"), was a particularly traumatic event, leading to the exile of approximately 100,000 Jews from the kingdom. This act, motivated by a combination of economic and religious factors, underscored the precariousness of Jewish life in France and the limitations of their integration into French society. Despite subsequent readmissions under varying conditions, the memory of these expulsions remained a powerful reminder of the vulnerability of the Jewish community.

The emergence of Portuguese Jewish (converso) communities in cities like Bayonne and Bordeaux further complicated the relationship between Jews and French identity. These communities, composed of descendants of Jews who had been forcibly converted to Christianity in Portugal and Spain, often maintained secret Jewish practices while outwardly adhering to Catholic norms. Their integration into French society was facilitated by their formal conversion, but their ambiguous religious status also created suspicion and tension.

The Sephardic and Ashkenazi communities developed distinct relationships to French identity. Sephardic Jews, often wealthier and more integrated into commercial networks, tended to embrace French culture and language, while maintaining their distinct religious and cultural traditions. Ashkenazi Jews, concentrated in Alsace and other regions of northeastern France, often maintained closer ties to Yiddish language and culture, and were more likely to adhere to traditional religious practices. These differences in background and orientation contributed to internal divisions within the French Jewish community, even as they shared a common experience of marginalization and discrimination.

**3. Emancipation (1791) and the "Israelite" Identity**

The French Revolution brought about a radical transformation in the status of Jews in France. The National Assembly's decree of September 27, 1791, granted Jews full citizenship rights, marking the first such emancipation in Europe. This momentous decision was driven by the Enlightenment ideals of equality and individual rights, as well as the belief that Jews could be integrated into French society as loyal citizens.

However, the emancipation came with certain expectations. As Stanislas de Clermont-Tonnerre famously declared, "To the Jews as a nation, nothing; to the Jews as individuals, everything." This formulation captured the prevailing view that Jews should be granted equal rights as individuals, but that their communal autonomy and distinct national identity should be relinquished in favor of assimilation into French society.

The concept of the "Israelite" identity emerged as a way for French Jews to reconcile their Jewish heritage with their French citizenship. This identity emphasized the religious aspects of Judaism while downplaying its national or cultural dimensions. French Jews were encouraged to see themselves as French citizens first and Jews second, prioritizing their loyalty to the French nation over any communal or international Jewish affiliations.

Napoleon Bonaparte further shaped the integration of Jews into French society through the creation of the Consistoire Central Israélite in 1806 and the convening of the Assembly of Jewish Notables and the Sanhedrin in 1807. These bodies were intended to represent the Jewish community and to ensure their adherence to French law and civic obligations. Napoleon's Sanhedrin sought to modernize Jewish law and practice in accordance with French values, further promoting the assimilation of Jews into French society.

**4. The Dreyfus Affair: A Crisis of Conscience**

The Dreyfus Affair (1894-1906) exposed the deep-seated antisemitism that persisted within French society, despite the formal emancipation of Jews. The wrongful conviction of Captain Alfred Dreyfus, a Jewish officer in the French army, on charges of treason sparked a national crisis that divided France along political and social lines.

Theodor Herzl, an Austrian Jewish journalist who covered the Dreyfus Affair, was profoundly affected by the antisemitism he witnessed in France. The public degradation of Dreyfus in 1895 convinced Herzl that assimilation was not a solution to the Jewish problem and that the creation of a Jewish state was necessary to ensure the safety and security of Jews. This experience led Herzl to develop his vision of political Zionism, which he articulated in his book *Der Judenstaat* (The Jewish State) in 1896.

Bernard Lazare, a French Jewish intellectual, was one of the first to publicly defend Dreyfus and to denounce the antisemitism that fueled the case. In his book *Antisémitisme, son histoire et ses causes* (Antisemitism, Its History and Causes) published in 1894, Lazare argued that antisemitism was a persistent feature of European history, rooted in social, economic, and religious factors.

The Dreyfus Affair also revealed divisions within the Jewish community itself. While many French Jews rallied to Dreyfus's defense, others were hesitant to challenge the established order or feared that doing so would further fuel antisemitism. This internal division reflected the complex and often contradictory pressures faced by French Jews as they navigated their dual identities as Jews and French citizens.

**5. The Shoah in France: Collaboration and Resistance**

The Vichy regime, established in France after the German occupation in 1940, actively collaborated with the Nazis in the persecution and deportation of Jews. The *Statut des Juifs* (October 3, 1940), drafted by French officials without direct German pressure, stripped Jews of their civil rights and excluded them from many professions. This legislation marked a turning point in the history of French Jewry, signaling the abandonment of the republican principles of equality and universalism.

The *Rafle du Vel d'Hiv* (Vel d'Hiv Roundup) on July 16-17, 1942, was a particularly horrific episode in the history of the Holocaust in France. French police rounded up over 13,000 Jews, including women and children, and detained them in the Vélodrome d'Hiver stadium in Paris before deporting them to Auschwitz. The active participation of French authorities in this operation demonstrated the extent of the Vichy regime's collaboration with the Nazis and the complicity of many French citizens in the persecution of Jews.

Despite the collaboration of the Vichy regime, there were also instances of resistance and solidarity. In the village of Le Chambon-sur-Lignon, led by Pastor André Trocmé and his wife Magda, the Protestant community sheltered thousands of Jewish refugees, providing them with food, shelter, and false documents. André and Magda Trocme were later recognized as Righteous Among the Nations by Yad Vashem for their courageous efforts to save Jewish lives. Countless other French families also risked their lives to hide Jewish children, demonstrating a spirit of human solidarity in the face of barbarism.

The ethical weight of the Shoah in France is undeniable. Out of approximately 76,000 Jews deported from France, only 2,500 returned. The Holocaust shattered the illusion of assimilation and revealed the fragility of Jewish life in France, leaving a profound and lasting impact on the French Jewish community.

**6. Postwar Jewish Philosophy: Levinas, Neher, Jankelevitch, Derrida**

The Holocaust profoundly influenced Jewish thought in France, giving rise to new ethical and philosophical perspectives.

Emmanuel Levinas (1906-1995), born in Lithuania and naturalized as French, developed an ethical philosophy that placed the "face of the Other" at the center of moral responsibility. In his seminal works, *Totalité et Infini* (Totality and Infinity, 1961) and *Autrement qu'être ou au-delà de l'essence* (Otherwise than Being or Beyond Essence, 1974), Levinas argued that ethics precedes ontology, meaning that our responsibility to others is prior to any philosophical system or concept of being. His Talmudic readings at the *Colloque des intellectuels juifs* (Colloquium of Jewish Intellectuals) informed his philosophical work, demonstrating the influence of Jewish tradition on his thinking. The face-to-face encounter with the Other, the vulnerability and call for help that it embodies, becomes the foundation for ethical obligation.

André Neher (1914-1988) explored the themes of exile and the relationship between language and revelation in his book *L'Exil de la Parole* (The Exile of the Word, 1970). Neher argued that the Holocaust represented a catastrophic break in the history of Western civilization, a moment when language itself was corrupted and rendered incapable of expressing the truth.

Vladimir Jankélévitch (1903-1985) grappled with the question of forgiveness in the aftermath of the Holocaust. In his essay "Pardonner?" ("Should We Pardon Them?", 1971), Jankélévitch argued that certain crimes, particularly those against humanity, are imprescriptible and unforgivable. He rejected the idea that time could heal the wounds of the Holocaust or that perpetrators could be absolved of their guilt, influencing later legal theory in France and beyond concerning crimes against humanity.

Jacques Derrida (1930-2004), born in Algeria to a Sephardic Jewish family, brought a colonial dimension to French Jewish identity. Derrida's experience as an Algerian Jew shaped his philosophical work, particularly his deconstruction of Western metaphysics and his critique of ethnocentrism. His work challenged the notion of a unified French identity and highlighted the experiences of marginalized and excluded groups, including Jews, within French society.

**7. Contemporary Challenges: Antisemitism, Emigration, and Identity**

Contemporary French Jewish life is marked by a number of challenges, including a rise in antisemitism, emigration to Israel, and ongoing debates about the relationship between French republican universalism and Jewish communal identity.

These attacks, often perpetrated by individuals with radical Islamist ideologies, have raised concerns about the safety and future of Jewish life in France.

In response to these challenges, many French Jews have chosen to emigrate to Israel, seeking a safer and more secure environment for themselves and their families. This emigration, known as *aliyah*, represents a significant demographic shift within the French Jewish community and reflects a growing disillusionment with French society.

Alain Finkielkraut, a prominent French Jewish intellectual, has evolved from a left-leaning critic of French society to a more conservative voice, expressing concern about the decline of French culture and the rise of multiculturalism. Finkielkraut's evolution reflects the complex and often contradictory pressures faced by French Jews as they navigate their dual identities as Jews and French citizens in an increasingly polarized society.

The ethical tension between French republican universalism and Jewish communal identity remains a central challenge for French Jews in the 21st century. How can Jews maintain their distinct religious and cultural traditions while fully participating in French society? This question continues to shape the debates and discussions within the French Jewish community and to influence their relationship with the broader French society.
