## *Kegare* Made Flesh — The *Burakumin* and the Ethics of Inherited Pollution

_This section traces the direct line from the Shinto purity/pollution framework described in the Foundations section to the structured caste discrimination that produced Japan's *burakumin* communities — and to the liberation movement that challenged it. It is the entry the Foundations section implies but does not complete._

---

### The Structural Consequence of *Kegare*

The Foundations section of this history describes *kegare* — ritual impurity, the state of defilement arising from contact with death, blood, and decay — as a central concept in Shinto ethics. What it does not say is where that concept landed in human lives.

The *burakumin* are where it landed.

During the Tokugawa period (1603–1868), Japan's feudal government formalized a rigid social hierarchy. At its base were communities whose occupations placed them in sustained contact with *kegare*: leather workers, butchers, executioners, undertakers, grave-tenders, sewage workers. These were not marginal trades. They were essential ones. The society required these people. It also required, by the logic of *kegare*, that they be marked as permanently defiled.

The formal term was *eta* — a word meaning, with deliberate cruelty, "full of filth." These communities were required to live in designated hamlets (*buraku*), wear prescribed clothing and hairstyles, observe curfews, and were barred from owning rice fields. The defilement was not individual. It was heritable. It was structural. The logic of *kegare* had been converted into a caste system.

The people of these communities came to be called *burakumin* — literally, "people of the hamlets."

---

### Emancipation That Changed Nothing

In 1871, three years after the Meiji Restoration, the new government issued the Emancipation Edict, formally abolishing the feudal caste system. The *eta* and *hinin* categories were legally dissolved. *Burakumin* were, on paper, equal citizens.

The discrimination did not dissolve. It relocated.

The Meiji state had modernized the legal framework while leaving the social infrastructure of *kegare* intact. *Burakumin* communities remained geographically concentrated in their historic hamlets. The *koseki* system — Japan's household registry, maintained continuously from the Meiji period — recorded family lineage in ways that made *buraku* ancestry traceable. Employers, landlords, and families arranging marriages used the *koseki* to screen for *buraku* origin. The legal category was gone. The social mechanism for enforcing it remained.

This is the pattern that scholars of Japanese discrimination return to repeatedly: formal legal equality coexisting with structural exclusion maintained through documentation, geography, and social knowledge. The *kegare* framework did not require explicit law to persist. It had been internalized deeply enough to reproduce itself without legal enforcement.

### The Suiheisha Declaration — Japan's First Human Rights Document

On March 3, 1922, in the Okazaki Public Hall in Kyoto, representatives of *buraku* communities from across Japan gathered to found the National Levelers' Association — the *Suiheisha*, from *suihei*, meaning "horizontal" or "level." The founding declaration they adopted that day is regarded as the first human rights declaration in Japanese history, and among the earliest such documents issued by a discriminated minority anywhere in Asia.

The declaration was drafted by Saiko Mankichi. Its language is worth reading in full, because it does something unusual: it reclaims the derogatory term rather than rejecting it.

> *The time has come when we can be proud of being Eta.*

This is not resignation. It is the same move that any liberation movement makes when it refuses the logic of the oppressor's naming — when it says: the shame you attached to this word belongs to you, not to us. The declaration continues:

> *We, who know just how cold human society can be, who know what it is to be pitied, do fervently seek and adore the warmth and light of human life from deep within our hearts.*

> *Let there be warmth in human society, let there be light in all human beings.*

The declaration explicitly criticized the "improvement movements" and "reconciliation movements" that had preceded it — charitable efforts organized by non-*buraku* people to improve *buraku* communities' hygiene and social habits. The founders of the *Suiheisha* rejected this framing precisely because it accepted the premise that *burakumin* were deficient and required improvement. They insisted instead on self-emancipation: liberation organized by the discriminated, not administered to them.

The *Suiheisha* found immediate solidarity with the *Hyeongpyengsa*, a parallel movement among the *baegjeong* — a similarly discriminated caste community in Korea under Japanese colonial rule. The two movements recognized each other's structural situation across national and linguistic lines. The *Suiheisha* Declaration was translated into Korean, Chinese, English, French, German, Russian, Spanish, and Ainu. There is a version in Braille.

### The Postwar Period — Dowa Policy and Its Limits

After World War II, the new Japanese constitution guaranteed equality regardless of "race, creed, sex, social status or family origin." Article 14 was explicit. The *burakumin* were, again, legally equal.

The discrimination persisted. A 1965 government report acknowledged it directly: "Buraku discrimination in modern society, in a word, is the violation of the civil rights and freedoms."

Between 1969 and 2002, the Japanese government implemented the *Dowa* (integration) policy — a series of affirmative measures in education, housing, and employment directed at *buraku* communities. Infrastructure in *buraku* areas was improved. Educational support programs were established. The material conditions of many *buraku* communities changed substantially.

What the *Dowa* policy could not address was the social mechanism. The *koseki* registry remained. Background check services — some operated by private detective agencies — continued to offer *buraku* district identification to employers and families. In 2009, when Google released a historical map overlay of Japan that included the locations of former *buraku* hamlets, the Japanese government and *buraku* organizations demanded its removal. Google complied. The argument was that making the geographic record publicly accessible would enable discrimination. The counterargument — that the discrimination was already happening through private channels, and that suppressing the record protected the discriminators more than the discriminated — was not resolved.

The *Dowa* policy expired in 2002. The formal affirmative measures ended. Discrimination in marriage and employment continues in attenuated form, documented in surveys conducted by the Buraku Liberation and Human Rights Research Institute. Hate speech targeting *burakumin* on Japanese internet platforms has increased in recent years.

### The Structural Argument

The *burakumin* case is not a historical anomaly. It is the demonstration case for a structural argument that runs through the entire Japan history:

The *kegare* framework — purity, pollution, the ethics of contamination — is not merely a religious concept. It is an allocative mechanism. It determines who bears the costs of necessary but stigmatized work. It determines who is marked as carrying those costs permanently. It determines whose children inherit the mark.

The Foundations section of this history describes *kegare* and *harae* as a system for "restoring harmony." That description is accurate as far as it goes. What it does not say is: harmony for whom. The *harae* ritual cleanses the person who has been contaminated. It does not address the social structure that required someone to do the contaminating work in the first place. The *burakumin* were the people the harmony was restored *from*.

This is the entry that a Japanese reader — particularly a *buraku* descendant, or a researcher in Japanese discrimination studies, or a person who has experienced the *koseki* check — would look for in a history of Japanese ethics. Not because it is the only story, but because it is the story the framework implies and then omits.

The Suiheisha Declaration closes: *Let there be warmth in human society, let there be light in all human beings.*

That line was written in 1922. It is still waiting.
