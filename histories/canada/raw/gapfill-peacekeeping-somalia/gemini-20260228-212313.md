### The Genesis of a Myth: Peacekeeping as National Identity

The idea of Canada as a "peacekeeper" is deeply embedded in the national narrative, particularly in contrast to the United States. This image took root during the Suez Crisis of 1956. When Egypt nationalized the Suez Canal, Britain, France, and Israel launched a military intervention. Then-Secretary of State for External Affairs Lester B. Pearson proposed the creation of the United Nations Emergency Force (UNEF I) to separate the warring parties. Pearson's plan allowed Britain, France, and Israel to withdraw while avoiding a potential escalation with the Soviet Union. He was awarded the Nobel Peace Prize in 1957 for his efforts. This cemented peacekeeping as central to Canadian identity.

This identity rested on a perceived ethical framework: Canada as a middle power, acting as an honest broker through multilateralism. This was reflected in government statements and public discourse. The idea was that Canada, unburdened by imperial ambitions and great power rivalries, could act as a neutral and trusted mediator in international conflicts.

### The Peacekeeping Record: Achievements and Risks

Canada actively participated in numerous peacekeeping missions, most notably in Cyprus (UNFICYP, 1964-) and on the Golan Heights (UNDOF). At its peak, Canada was among the largest contributors of personnel to UN peacekeeping operations. These missions involved genuine achievements, such as preventing further escalation of conflicts and providing humanitarian assistance. However, peacekeeping was never without risks. Canadian peacekeepers faced dangerous situations and sustained casualties. While the official narrative emphasized the peaceful nature of these missions, peacekeepers often operated in environments characterized by violence and instability.

### Rwanda: The Ethical Abyss

The Rwandan genocide of 1994 exposed the limitations and ethical failures of the peacekeeping framework. Canadian General Romeo Dallaire commanded the United Nations Assistance Mission for Rwanda (UNAMIR). He witnessed the systematic slaughter of approximately 800,000 Tutsi and moderate Hutu. Dallaire's repeated requests for reinforcements and a stronger mandate were denied by the UN Security Council. His famous fax to UN headquarters, warning of the impending genocide, was ignored. This exposed the hypocrisy of the international community and the inadequacy of the peacekeeping mandate in the face of mass atrocities.

Dallaire's personal experience and his subsequent book, *Shake Hands with the Devil: The Failure of Humanity in Rwanda* (2003), became a landmark Canadian ethical document. It detailed the horrors he witnessed, the bureaucratic obstacles he faced, and his own descent into post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD). Dallaire's story demonstrated the immense ethical cost borne by peacekeepers who are forced to stand by while atrocities occur. His subsequent advocacy for the prevention of genocide and the improvement of mental health support for veterans highlights the long-term consequences of ethical failure in peacekeeping.

The ethical crisis triggered by Rwanda and Somalia led to a shift in Canadian foreign policy away from traditional peacekeeping and towards "peace enforcement" and combat roles. This shift raised fundamental ethical questions about Canada's role in the world. Was Canada abandoning its commitment to multilateralism and peaceful conflict resolution in favor of a more aggressive and interventionist foreign policy? The rise in PTSD cases among returning veterans, and the challenges in providing adequate mental health support, further underscored the ethical costs of Canada's involvement in combat operations.
