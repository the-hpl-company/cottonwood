**Babylonian Ethics: An Evolution from Sumerian Precedents**

The Babylonian ethical tradition, while deeply rooted in Sumerian thought, represents a significant evolution. The Abrahamic traditions inherited from this Babylonian context, not directly from the earlier Sumerian.

**1. The Code of Hammurabi**

*   **Context:** The Code of Hammurabi, dating to ~1754 BCE, is one of the most complete surviving legal codes from the ancient Near East. It is inscribed on a diorite stele and presents a comprehensive set of laws governing various aspects of Babylonian life.
*   **Comparison with Ur-Nammu:** While both the Code of Ur-Nammu (c. 2100-2050 BCE) and the Code of Hammurabi aim to establish justice and order, there are crucial differences. Ur-Nammu's code, fragmentary as it is, emphasizes monetary compensation and restitution for offenses. Hammurabi's Code, on the other hand, frequently employs the principle of *lex talionis* (the law of retaliation, "an eye for an eye").
    *   **Preservation:** Both codes reflect a concern for protecting the vulnerable (widows, orphans) and regulating economic transactions. The idea of a king as a guarantor of justice is also preserved.
    *   **Change: *Lex Talionis***: The shift to *lex talionis* in Hammurabi's Code has profound ethical implications.
        *   **Restitution:** Restitution aims to restore balance and repair harm by compensating the victim. It emphasizes the economic and social consequences of wrongdoing.
        *   ***Lex Talionis***: *Lex talionis* focuses on punishment as a form of retribution. It reflects a more punitive and potentially vengeful approach to justice. Ethically, it raises questions about proportionality and the cycle of violence. As philosopher Martha Nussbaum argues, the focus on retribution can obscure the underlying causes of crime and neglect the needs of victims.
    *   **Class-Based Justice:** Hammurabi's Code notoriously operates on a stratified social system, dividing society into *awilum* (free persons, upper class), *mushkenum* (commoners), and *wardum* (slaves). The severity of punishment and the amount of compensation often depended on the social status of both the victim and the perpetrator.
        *   Example: If an *awilum* injured another *awilum*, the principle of *lex talionis* might apply directly. However, if an *awilum* injured a *mushkenum*, the compensation would be significantly lower. The code considers *wardum* property with limited rights, and injury to them is addressed primarily as damage to their owner's property.
        *   **Ethical Implications:** This class-based system profoundly shapes the ethics of harm. It demonstrates that justice was not blind, but rather deeply influenced by social hierarchy. This creates an ethical framework where some lives are valued more than others, and where the powerful are less accountable for their actions than the powerless.
        *   As Jean Bottéro notes, the Code reflects a society deeply concerned with maintaining order and control, even at the expense of equality.

**2. The Epic of Gilgamesh**

*   **Context:** The Epic of Gilgamesh, existing in various versions from the Old Babylonian period (c. 1800 BCE) onward, is a cornerstone of Mesopotamian literature and a profound exploration of mortality, friendship, and the search for meaning.
*   **Gilgamesh's Transformation:** The epic traces Gilgamesh's journey from a tyrannical ruler to a wise and compassionate leader.
    *   Initially, Gilgamesh is depicted as an oppressive king who abuses his power, exhausting his people with forced labor and sexual exploitation (Tablet I).
    *   The death of Enkidu, his friend and companion, is the catalyst for his transformation. Confronted with the reality of mortality, Gilgamesh embarks on a quest for immortality.
    *   His journey leads him to Utnapishtim, the survivor of the great flood, who reveals the story of the deluge and the limits of human ambition.
*   **Enkidu's Death:** Enkidu's death forces Gilgamesh to confront his own mortality. This confrontation leads to a profound ethical shift. He realizes that true meaning is not found in endless life, but in living a life of purpose and leaving a lasting legacy through his accomplishments and his care for his people.
*   **Utnapishtim and the Flood:** Utnapishtim's tale of the flood is a direct precursor to the biblical story of Noah. The Babylonian version, however, differs in key aspects:
    *   The reasons for the flood vary. In Gilgamesh, the gods decide to destroy humanity because they are too noisy. In Genesis, God is grieved by human wickedness (Genesis 6:5-7).
    *   The character of the flood hero differs. Utnapishtim is granted immortality for his role in saving humanity. Noah is portrayed as a righteous man who obeys God's commands.
*   **Ethical Implications of Mortality:** Utnapishtim tells Gilgamesh: "When the gods created mankind, death for mankind they set aside, life in their own hands retaining" (Tablet X). This statement has significant ethical implications:
    *   It emphasizes the limitations of human existence and the futility of seeking immortality through purely physical means.
    *   It suggests that human beings should focus on making the most of their finite lives by pursuing wisdom, justice, and compassion.
    *   It highlights the importance of legacy – the impact one has on the world during their lifetime.
*   Stephanie Dalley's translation and commentary provide valuable insights into the epic's literary and cultural context. Andrew George's work on the textual history of Gilgamesh is also essential.

**3. The Enuma Elish**

*   **Context:** The Enuma Elish ("When on High"), a Babylonian creation epic, narrates the rise of the god Marduk and the creation of the world from the primordial waters.
*   **Differences from Sumerian Versions:** Sumerian creation myths (such as the Eridu Genesis) typically involve a more decentralized pantheon and focus on the creation of humans to serve the gods. The Enuma Elish, in its Babylonian form, elevates Marduk to the supreme position.
    *   In the Enuma Elish, Marduk defeats the primordial goddess Tiamat and creates the world from her body. He then creates humanity from the blood of Kingu, Tiamat's consort.
*   **Marduk's Elevation and Ethics of Authority:** Marduk's elevation has significant implications for the ethics of authority:
    *   It establishes a hierarchical order in the cosmos, with Marduk at the top. This hierarchy is reflected in Babylonian society, where the king is seen as Marduk's representative on Earth.
    *   It legitimizes Marduk's rule through his victory over chaos. This suggests that power and authority are justified by the ability to maintain order and protect the community.
    *   It implies that human beings have a duty to serve Marduk and uphold his cosmic order.
*   **Ethical Questions:** While legitimizing authority, the *Enuma Elish* raises questions about divine violence and the nature of power. Is Marduk's rule just simply because he is powerful? Does the end (creation of order) justify the means (violent conquest)?
*   W.G. Lambert's analysis of Babylonian creation myths sheds light on the evolving religious and political landscape of Mesopotamia.

**4. Babylonian Theodicy**

*   **Context:** Babylonian literature grapples with the problem of theodicy – the question of why righteous people suffer. Two key texts illustrate this struggle: "The Babylonian Theodicy" (also known as "A Dialogue About Human Misery") and "Ludlul bēl nēmeqi" ("I Will Praise the Lord of Wisdom").
*   **"The Babylonian Theodicy":** This poem presents a dialogue between a sufferer and his friend. The sufferer laments his misfortunes, despite his piety and adherence to religious rituals. His friend struggles to explain why he is suffering, offering conventional explanations that ultimately prove unsatisfactory.
    *   The friend initially argues that the gods are inscrutable and that human understanding is limited. He also suggests that the sufferer may have unknowingly committed some sin. However, the sufferer rejects these explanations, insisting on his innocence.
    *   The poem ends without a clear resolution, suggesting that the problem of innocent suffering is ultimately a mystery.
*   **"Ludlul bēl nēmeqi":** This text, also known as the "Poem of the Righteous Sufferer," recounts the misfortunes of a high-ranking official named Ludlul. He suffers from illness, social ostracism, and the loss of his possessions, despite his devotion to the gods.
    *   Ludlul's suffering leads him to question the justice of the gods and the meaning of life. He eventually experiences a vision in which the god Marduk appears to him and restores him to health and prosperity.
    *   The poem concludes with Ludlul praising Marduk for his mercy and acknowledging the limits of human understanding.
*   **Comparison with Job:** The Babylonian theodical texts bear striking similarities to the biblical Book of Job:
    *   Both Job and the Babylonian sufferers are righteous individuals who experience inexplicable suffering.
    *   Both texts challenge conventional explanations of suffering and question the justice of the divine order.
    *   Both texts ultimately affirm the power and wisdom of God, even in the face of unanswered questions.
    *   However, there are also differences. Job is tested by Satan with God's permission, while the Babylonian texts do not explicitly mention such a figure. The Book of Job also places more emphasis on the importance of maintaining faith in the face of adversity.
*   **Ethical Implications:** Babylonian theodicy reflects a growing awareness of the complexities of the moral universe. It challenges the notion that piety always leads to prosperity and raises questions about the nature of divine justice. It also emphasizes the importance of humility and the acceptance of human limitations.

**5. Transmission Mechanism: The Babylonian Captivity**

*   **Context:** The Babylonian Captivity (597-539 BCE) was a period in Jewish history when a significant number of Judeans were exiled to Babylon following the Babylonian conquest of Jerusalem. This period of exile served as a critical point of cultural and intellectual exchange between the Babylonians and the Israelites.
*   **Impact:** During the exile, the Israelites were exposed to Babylonian literature, mythology, law, and religious practices. This exposure had a profound impact on the development of Israelite thought.
*   **Absorption into Israelite Thought:** Scholars believe that the Israelites absorbed several key elements of Babylonian culture during the exile:
    *   **Cosmology:** The Babylonian creation myth (Enuma Elish) influenced the development of the creation account in Genesis 1. The concept of a primordial watery chaos and the division of the waters are both present in both accounts.
    *   **Flood Narrative:** The Babylonian flood story (Epic of Gilgamesh) served as a direct source for the biblical flood narrative in Genesis 6-9. Similarities include the divine decision to destroy humanity, the construction of an ark, the sending out of birds to test the waters, and the offering of sacrifices after the flood.
    *   **Legal Codes:** Some scholars argue that the Code of Hammurabi influenced the development of Israelite law, particularly the Covenant Code in Exodus 20-23. Similarities include laws regarding theft, assault, and property damage.
    *   **Literary Forms:** Babylonian literary forms, such as lamentations and wisdom literature, influenced the development of similar genres in the Hebrew Bible.

**6. Textual Parallels**

*   **Flood Narratives:**
    *   *Epic of Gilgamesh*, Tablet XI and *Genesis* 6-9. Similarities include: divine decision to flood the earth, construction of an ark, sending out birds to test for dry land, and offering sacrifices after the flood.
*   **Creation Accounts:**
    *   *Enuma Elish* and *Genesis* 1. Similarities include: primordial watery chaos, division of waters, creation of light, and creation of humans as the last act.
*   **Legal Codes:**
    *   *Code of Hammurabi* and *Exodus* 21-23 (Covenant Code). Similarities include: laws regarding assault, theft, property damage, and treatment of slaves. Specific parallels include laws concerning *lex talionis* ("eye for an eye") and compensation for injuries.

**7. Key Scholars and Contested Interpretations**

*   **Stephanie Dalley:** Known for her translations and interpretations of Mesopotamian literature, including the Epic of Gilgamesh. Her work emphasizes the literary and artistic qualities of these texts.
*   **Andrew George:** A leading expert on the Epic of Gilgamesh, focusing on its textual history and transmission.
*   **W.G. Lambert:** A prominent scholar of Babylonian religion and literature. His work explores the relationship between Babylonian mythology and ritual.
*   **Jean Bottéro:** A renowned historian of the ancient Near East. His work examines the social, economic, and political context of Mesopotamian civilization.
*   **Contested Interpretations:**
    *   The extent of Babylonian influence on the Hebrew Bible is a subject of ongoing debate. Some scholars argue for a direct and substantial influence, while others emphasize the distinctiveness of Israelite religion and culture.
    *   The interpretation of *lex talionis* in the Code of Hammurabi is also contested. Some scholars argue that it was intended to be applied literally, while others believe that it was often commuted to monetary compensation.
    *   The dating and authorship of various Mesopotamian texts are also subjects of scholarly debate.

In summary, the Babylonian ethical tradition built upon Sumerian foundations but introduced significant changes, particularly in the areas of law, theodicy, and the concept of divine authority. These developments, transmitted through the Babylonian Captivity, profoundly shaped the development of Israelite thought and the Abrahamic traditions.
